Field Notes / Cobalt

The world has always run more equations than it has bothered to name.

A black wire arcs over a graded apron in a small Northern Ontario town. The dashcam records it. What you are looking at is not the wire — it is the wire multiplied through a lens, which is itself running an equation no one in the document acknowledges. Toggle the layers; watch the bundle decompose.

Galileo called the catenary a parabola and was wrong; Hooke had it right but encrypted his solution as an anagram in case he wasn't; Bernoulli, Huygens and Leibniz finally named it in 1691. Before that year every chain on every wall was performing a piece of math no one on earth had a name for.

The dashcam barrel was named — by someone, in a memo somewhere — but the name has not made it out of the supplier's archive. The decision affects a generation of documentary footage. The decomposer is what you have to build to argue with it.
