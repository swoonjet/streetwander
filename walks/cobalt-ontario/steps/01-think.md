SEED:
- a long low guardrail running the lip of an embankment, its top edge catching a pale skim of sky
- a graded dirt apron in the foreground — flat, blank, recently scraped, no markings
- a water tower silhouetted on the far left ridge, its bulb floating above the treeline like a punctuation mark

WANDER:
The graded apron is the most interesting nothing in the frame. It's the surface a town leaves behind when it has stopped being sure what to put on a piece of ground — gravel-flat, drained, the texture of a place that's been *prepared* without being *committed*. Mining towns are full of this: the post-extraction lull where the headframe is gone, the pit is fenced, the rail still runs, and what's left is a planar zone waiting for an idea. I think of it as the urban equivalent of a primed canvas, and primed canvas is itself a strange object — it's not a painting, it's a *promise* that respects the substrate enough to flatten it. Robert Ryman built a forty-year career on the threshold between primed and painted, and the moral of that career is that a surface in waiting is already taking a position. So the apron is taking a position, even if no one in Cobalt is making that argument out loud. There's a parallel in software: the empty state. The first screen of a freshly-installed app, before any data is in it, is doing more rhetorical work than the loaded screen ever will, because the empty state has to teach you what *kind of thing* the app intends to be while pretending to just sit there. The apron is an empty state with weather. The dirt remembers what was scraped off it but doesn't quite know what's coming. Maybe what's coming is parking. Maybe nothing — and that's also a use.

BRANCH:
The "primed-but-not-painted" condition as a civic posture — places that have intentionally stopped, and how that differs from places that have merely paused.

ECHO:
Extends step 0's thread about declarations versus what they're carrying — a false front claims a building it doesn't have; a graded apron declines to claim anything yet, and that refusal is its own kind of statement. Both are surfaces performing a relationship to a future that may or may not arrive.
