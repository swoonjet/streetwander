WANDER:
The bald patch in the treeline is doing the work. Around Cobalt those gaps are rarely natural — silver mining left tailings whose metals (cobalt itself, arsenic, mercury, nickel) keep particular patches of ground unwilling to host a forest fifty, eighty, a hundred years after the ore left. So the canopy is a map written in green, and what's *missing* from the map is the readable part: absence is the signal. This inverts the usual figure-ground assumption — that what's there is information and what's not there is field — and it's a much commoner inversion than we admit. The counter inside a Bodoni *a* carries more identity than the stroke; the rest in a Mingus head does the swing; the gap in an aerial photograph where barley grows half a foot shorter is the Roman ditch the field forgot it was hiding. Forensic landscape archaeology has known this for a century — vegetation differentials are the patient reader of buried events — and LiDAR mostly industrialized the principle by stripping the canopy in software, so the Mayan causeways could finish their long argument with the rainforest. The Cobalt version of this argument is slower and conducted in plants: the metallophytes that tolerate heavy-metal soil (the *Silene vulgaris* of mine spoil, sea-thrift of zinc tips in Wales) colonize first, and a botanist can read prior contamination by which species got there in what order. The forest is doing geochemistry in a vegetal alphabet on a publication schedule of about a century per word, and the bald patch is a footnote that hasn't been written yet.

BRANCH:
Who is licensed to read a landscape like that — the geologist, the ecologist, the descendant of the people who lived there before the mine — and whether the readings reconcile.

ECHO:
Extends the previous two steps but turns the surface around: the false front and the graded apron were each declaring something to a future that may not arrive; this hillside is the opposite — an *involuntary* inscription, recording a past that the place would mostly prefer to grow over. Declared futures vs. inadvertent pasts, both legible in the ground.
