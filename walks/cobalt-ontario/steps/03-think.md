WANDER:
The frame is mostly sky, and that ratio is misleading me. Cobalt sits on the southern lip of the Canadian Shield — a township east, the boreal closes back over the road like a zipper — but a hundred-twenty years of underground silver, surface tailings, settlement clearance, mill grounds, rail right-of-way and post-bust quiet has scraped the local skyline into proportions that read more like prairie than shield, and the eye obediently reaches for the prairie file. Subtraction can make a place rhyme with categories it has no biographical claim to. There's a parallel in linguistics: when a creole forms in a port under pressure, the grammar simplifies so fast that the result often looks structurally like another, unrelated creole on a different ocean — Tok Pisin and Haitian Kreyòl converging on similar tense markers not from common descent but because the forces that shaped them (urgent contact, mixed inputs, fluent-second-language adult speakers) have a common signature. Pressure flattens toward shared shapes. The same convergence runs the other way in a Vermont valley grown shaggy with secondary forest after the sheep economy collapsed in the 1880s — it looks more like an Appalachian hollow now than the scoured pasture it actually was, because abandonment, like pidginization, reaches for a default. In both cases the place ends up speaking a vernacular it didn't inherit. So a visitor looking at this much sky is half-correctly thinking *open country* and half-incorrectly being told the wrong story by their eyes; the ratio is honest about the present and dishonest about the lineage. Worth distinguishing this from the themed convergence — Paris-on-the-Strip, the Bavarian village in Georgia — where the resemblance is *staged*, expensive, and contractual. Convergence by pressure is cheap and unsupervised, and it produces the more durable confusion, because nobody has to maintain it.
