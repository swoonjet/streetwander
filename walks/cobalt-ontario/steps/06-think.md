WANDER:
The wire is running two equations at once and only one of them is the planet's. A cable hung slack between two poles draws a catenary — y = a·cosh(x/a) — which Galileo got wrong (he called it a parabola, and the two are close enough at low sag that his mistake survived sixty-odd years), Hooke claimed correctly but encrypted as an anagram in case he was wrong, and Bernoulli, Huygens and Leibniz finally co-solved in 1691. Which means that before that year every chain on every wall, every loose rope, every telegraph wire still in the future, was performing a piece of math no one on earth had a name for — and I find that genuinely moving, the world full of unnamed equations doing their work whether or not anyone has caught up. Gaudí caught up two centuries later in the most physical way available: he hung weighted strings upside-down in the Colònia Güell crypt model, photographed the result, inverted the photo, and read the masonry compression line for free, letting gravity *find* the building rather than drawing it. Now look back at the wire over Cobalt — it's faithfully running cosh, but the dashcam's barrel projection is composited on top, probably an equidistant mapping where angle and radius scale linearly, so what reads as "the curve of the wire" is two unrelated functions multiplied through one black line; the eye can't decompose them, it integrates and calls the result a shape. Most appearances are this kind of multiplication, actually — a perceived signal is a stack of equations no one disentangles in real time, and the only reason we trust any of it is a convention quietly agreeing on which layers to ignore. We do it in audio with the room's transfer function (pretending the speaker has a flat response when actually we're hearing speaker × room × ear-canal × familiarity-bias), we do it in paint by treating the gallery wall's albedo as a constant, we do it in interface design when a designer specs a hex value and ships a button whose final color is hex × OS-rendered drop shadow × monitor gamut × ambient room light, and we mostly call the result the hex value because the alternative is unbearable.
