WANDER:
A water tower is the simplest possible bet against time — lift water once, store it high, let gravity do the distribution forever after, a stored future that smooths the difference between when the pump runs and when the tap is turned. Buffers are everywhere infrastructure has had to negotiate with mismatched rhythms: granaries, batteries, reservoirs, the supermarket back room, the L1 cache between a CPU and its RAM, the unread inbox between arrival and attention. The interesting story of the last forty years is that buffers keep dying because the source kept speeding up — variable-frequency pumps with pressurized mains made the tower redundant, just-in-time gutted the warehouse, streaming killed the local download, and microservices replaced the message queue with the synchronous call, until somebody noticed the queue had been the only thing absorbing failure gracefully and we started reinventing it under names like "event sourcing." So the bulb on stilts is decommissioned ideology as much as decommissioned hardware: it argues, silently, that you should *plan for* the gap between supply and demand instead of engineering it out. Buffers are slack, and the modern instinct toward leanness reads slack as waste — true on a spreadsheet, false on the day a pump fails. Covid was a long international tutorial on what slack was for, paid in masks and ICU beds, and within two years the lesson had mostly faded; sovereign grain reserves still aren't fashionable, and the same pitch decks are again valorizing the elimination of inventory. You could write a quiet history of civilization through how generously each century stored its tomorrow — the Roman *horrea*, the Inka *qollqa* terraces with their north-facing ventilation, the Edo-era rice warehouses at Dōjima, the New York City watershed reservoirs that are essentially a hundred-mile horizontal water tower — and Cobalt's small example is still standing not because it's still in service but because nobody has gotten around to disagreeing with it yet by knocking it down.

BRANCH:
Whether replacing physical buffers with software queues counts as progress or as slack relocated — moved from concrete to abstraction, from civic asset to private dependency, where it becomes harder to see and easier to delete in the next outage.
