WANDER:
The rail curve in the foreground is doing geometry the rest of the landscape isn't required to do. Rail has a minimum-radius problem — a freight train can't take a turn tighter than maybe 90 meters without flange-bind and wheel squeal, and most mainline curves are cut to two or three times that — so you can read a transport mode out of the *vocabulary of curves* it's allowed to use. Roads hairpin; trains can't. Canals follow contours or lock; pipelines mostly go straight and pay for the trench. Each right-of-way is a sentence written in its native grammar, and you can sometimes identify the speaker just from the syntax — the Roman road's straight-through-anything is a different language than the medieval cart track's deference to slope, and a Mughal water-channel running gradient-true to the inch answers to a third grammar again. The parallel I keep coming back to is programming languages: each admits some expressions cheaply and resists others. APL composes vector operations in a single glyphic line and can't pretty-print itself, like a canal honoring contour. SQL is rail — you declare the destination, the query planner picks the alignment, and it optimizes within a fixed grammar of joins and scans. Python is the road network: expressive everywhere, fastest nowhere. Lisp lets you grade your own path through the macro system, which makes it Roman, and the people who love it have a Roman attitude about it. The useful property of a constrained grammar is that it makes the easy moves legible — you can read someone fluent in SQL because their joins fall into expected sweeps — and it makes the unusual moves expensive: a tighter-than-minimum rail curve is built deliberately, with signals and reduced limits, and a recursive CTE is its software cousin, slower and louder by the same logic. So the gentle arc across this lot is just rail being grammatical; the power line strung casually above it is the opposite, a grammar that admits hairpins at every pole and cantilevers across a landscape it doesn't have to honor. Two infrastructures cohabiting one valley, speaking past each other in compatible silence.

BRANCH:
Designing a city by deliberately mixing infrastructural grammars — committing the rail-like ones to long-life decisions and reserving the power-line-like ones for things you expect to revise — and whether civic planning has ever consciously thought in those terms or only ever stumbled into the mix.
