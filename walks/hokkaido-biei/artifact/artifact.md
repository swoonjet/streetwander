# A Glossary of Frozen Verbs

*A field lexicon, entries collected out of doors. The conceit: most objects in a public place are verbs that slowed until they looked still. This glossary returns them, briefly, to verb form.*

---

**andon**, v.
To preserve a human hand inside a machine that no longer needs one. After the cord on the Toyota assembly line, which any worker may pull to halt production — production halted not because a sensor tripped but because a person chose to halt it. To andon a process is to spend money keeping someone legible inside it. See *flagger*.

**cobra-head**, v.
To bend a civic object, for seventy years, toward a name that was always wrong for it. The streetlight in question is a gooseneck — a drafting lamp leaned over a page — but the trade press in the 1950s called it a cobra, and every redesign since has quietly refined toward menace rather than attention. Naming back-propagates. Cf. *Segway*, a pun on *segue* that delivered a transition when the market wanted an arrival.

**cycle**, v. *(of a signal, for an empty intersection)*
To perform an office when no congregation is present. The light at 3 a.m. continues: red, amber, green. The muezzin calls over a sleeping city. The BBC shipping forecast reads sea areas to trawlers long since replaced by GPS. The Swiss Guard changes at posts the Pope hasn't walked past in weeks. Availability costs the same whether anyone shows up or not. The uselessness is not the point; the *readiness* is, and readiness is the difference between a city and a set.

**fence**, v. *(vernacular sense)*
To produce a form no one authored and everyone agrees to build. Two-inch galvanized pipe, welded into a waist-high two-rail, painted white: the form arrived by convergence, the way the Windsor chair did, and now edges every ditch and schoolyard in a country without a credited designer. The verb is collective, anonymous, and unsigned — and it does, by order of magnitude, more work in a streetscape than any authored gesture. See also *what-the-contractor-had-on-the-truck*.

**flagger**, v.
To embody an apology that a cone could just as well hold. The person in the hi-vis vest is not directing traffic — a sign directs traffic — the person is the receipt for the inconvenience of your slowing. Related: *andon*, *attend*. Antonym, in spirit: *cone*, which shouts because it cannot bow.

**hoverfly**, v.
To borrow a wasp's credibility without its cost. Hazard stripes wrapped around a concrete pole that cannot bite; yellow tape around a philodendron in a hotel lobby; the amber rotating beacon atop a parked golf cart. The signal is correct, the threat is absent, and the visual commons pays a small tax each time. Run long enough, the wasp pattern greys out for everyone. See also *inflation*.

**kitty**, v.
To bolt a nearly-featureless face onto a civic object, so that the signal softens without committing to a mood. From the Hello Kitty barricade: the absent mouth is load-bearing. A face, once present, cannot be abstracted; a row of barricades becomes not a pattern but a crowd. The Pareidolia Tax — every designer pays it the moment they put eyes on something.

**nobori**, v.
To twitch. To prove liveness the way a blinking cursor does, by exchanging content for pulse. The vertical banner flutters not because vertical is prettier but because wind catches the hem for free; the ancestor was a Sengoku battlefield marker, and the descendant is the inflatable tube man outside an American dealership — content-free, kinetic, and an answer to the only question that matters on a retail street, which is *is anyone here*. See also: prayer flags fraying at the weave (the fraying is the blessing going out), the barber pole's impossibly-rising stripe, kettle-steam at a second-floor window.

**pollard**, v.
To keep a shape by returning a saw to the same knuckles each winter. The tree becomes a log of its own maintenance — stratigraphy in wood — and a codebase refactored quarterly has the same candelabra profile, knuckles where the old interface was pruned back and fresh modules sprouting from each scar. Everything that holds a shape against entropy is paying in recurring labor, and the shape is secretly a schedule. Cf. *rake* (as at Ryōan-ji, daily), *mow*, *haircut*, *manicure*, *repaint*.

**shakkei**, v. *(lit. borrowed scenery)*
To frame a mountain one does not own and consider it borrowed. The Zen garden's rear hedge is cut to admit, not exclude, a distant peak; the image is completed by a geography the gardener never paid for. Every cultivated view is a negotiation with an offstage collaborator. The verb applies, quietly, to any composition that relies on what it cannot command — a photograph that needs its weather, a restaurant that needs its neighborhood, a building that needs the light of a particular latitude.

**shichijūni-kō**, v.
To fold seventy-two micro-seasons into four. Japan natively kept a calendar of five-day units, each with a poem for a title — *insects stir in the warmth*, *frost falls, the maples yellow* — and the export grade, "Four Seasons," is what a Vivaldi-trained ear can buy. Every time a calendar is compressed for sale, the residue is the same: the micro-seasons don't die, they get folded and shipped like luggage. The verb is the folding.

**stoep**, v.
To reach one pace out of the house and hold it. The raised half-step of masonry in front of a Dutch door, scrubbed daily; the *uchimizu* water sprinkled at dusk in front of a Kyoto shopfront; the Milanese widow setting a chair out in August. The sidewalk is not binary. The gradient has to be performed or it disappears.

**tulip**, v.
To set a small pot of flowers at the edge of pavement and water it at dusk. A private transaction with public ground. The urban planner's term is *frontage activation*; the planner cannot permit it into existence, because it is the residue of a street already slow enough that a person finds themselves wanting to. To legislate tulipping is a category error. See *stoep*.

**zebra**, v.
To paint a right back onto a road after losing the assumption of it. Slough, 1949: pedestrian priority became legible only once it was striped onto the asphalt, because it was no longer understood in the body. The crosswalk is an index of a surrender. Every painted line is a ghost of something a culture used to know without having to draw.

---

*Entries compiled without agreement. Cross-references are suggestive, not binding. The absence of any headword for* **cone** *is deliberate: the cone is a shout and does not inflect.*

CLOSING:

The walk kept circling one question — what in a streetscape is a noun, and what is a verb that has been still long enough to look like one? A glossary performs the question rather than arguing it: each entry freezes a verb into dictionary form, which is exactly the move the walk was naming. Prose would have lectured; a diagram would have flattened; this format lets the reader do the reanimation.
