MOMENTS:
- The question the walk kept returning to: what in a streetscape is a noun, and what is secretly a verb that has been still long enough to look like one? (pollarded tree, pipe fence, tulip in a planter, flagger with a flag)
- A related one: civic liveness as proof-of-life — the nobori twitching, the traffic light cycling at 3am for an empty intersection, the flagger standing in as the body of the apology. Availability costs the same whether anyone shows up or not.
- Underneath both: design discourse pays attention to the authored fraction and ignores the enormous vernacular substrate (white pipe fence, cobra-head lamp, whatever the contractor had on the truck) that actually carries the city.

MEDIUM:
An invented field lexicon — "A Glossary of Frozen Verbs" — short definitional entries treating public-space objects as verbs that slowed until they looked still.

REASONING:
The walk wasn't really about Hokkaido; by step 13 it was explicitly chasing the idea that objects-in-a-street are mostly schedules pretending to be things, and the nobori in step 14 locked that in from the other side (a verb that refused to slow). A glossary format lets the artifact *perform* the thesis — each entry freezes a verb into dictionary form, which is exactly the trick the walk was naming. Prose would lecture; a diagram would flatten; a lexicon enacts.
