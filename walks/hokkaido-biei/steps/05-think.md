- A row of spruce or fir trees along the left sidewalk, too uniform and evenly-spaced to be wild — planted, probably decades ago, as a deliberately "northern" ornamental choice
- The TV antenna on the white house at right, rabbit-ear branches rising into empty sky — a receiver still pointed at a frequency that may no longer be broadcasting

WANDER:
The tulip planter is someone's decision. Someone carried it out from a kitchen doorway, set it on the line between sidewalk and road, and is probably watering it at dusk — a small private transaction with public ground. Kyoto shopkeepers have done a version of this for centuries with *uchimizu*, the morning and evening sprinkling of water in front of the storefront, ostensibly to settle dust but actually to mark a meter of pavement as cared-for, as belonging-to-the-shop-without-being-the-shop's. The Dutch *stoep* is the inverse move in the same register — a raised half-step of masonry in front of the house, legally the city's but scrubbed daily by the homeowner, a threshold where the domestic reaches one pace into the civic and holds it. What these share is the recognition that the sidewalk isn't binary: the Anglo-American street assumes a clean legal edge — property line, pavement, road, three strips that don't talk to each other — and the planter, the uchimizu, the stoep, the Milanese widow putting her chair outside in August all insist that the edge is actually a gradient, and that the gradient has to be *performed* or it disappears. Which is maybe why when cities try to legislate "frontage activation" — Melbourne's laneway program, Barcelona's superblocks — they keep rediscovering that the thing they're trying to manufacture was always a byproduct of someone just being there, sweeping, setting out a chair, watering a pot. You can't permit a tulip. You can only make a street slow enough and narrow enough and cared-about enough that a person finds themselves wanting to put one there. Tokyo's shitamachi alleys are thick with this — micro-gardens overflowing onto the lane, potted trees claiming thirty centimeters of asphalt, an old bicycle leaning against a wall with morning glories climbing it — and the civic theorists call it *encroachment* while the residents call it *gardening*, which tells you exactly whose language is doing the accounting.

BRANCH:
The planted row of spruces itself — an imported Meiji/Taisho-era landscaping move, "northern" as a national performance rather than as a local ecology. The idea that a streetscape can be in the wrong accent even when every tree is healthy.

ECHO:
Extends step 4's thread. The flagger is the body a culture keeps inside a public-works operation; the tulip is the body a resident keeps inside the sidewalk. Both are refusals of the abstraction that would let a cone or a paving stone do the job alone — civic legibility paid for, in both cases, by someone choosing to be present.
