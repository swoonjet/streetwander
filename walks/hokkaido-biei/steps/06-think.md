- The small blue-and-red circular no-stopping sign bolted to the same pole, subordinate, a footnote to the header
- Another flagger further down the block, red flag low by his thigh — the third one I've registered in ten minutes

WANDER:
The sign points you to the Tower of the Four Seasons. That's a strange load to hang on a building — a monument is supposed to be legible in one visit, and this one has conceded, in its name, that the spec has failed before construction. You can't read it in August. You can't read it in February. You can only read it by returning, which means the object is secretly a subscription: pay four visits minimum, receive the full product. Buildings-as-SaaS is less outrageous a frame than it sounds. Ise Jingū gets torn down and rebuilt every twenty years and what's preserved is not the wood but the reading-over-time; the Shikoku 88 pilgrimage is a structure completed across weeks, the individual temples near-meaningless without the loop around them; Kenrokuen is openly a different garden in snow than in plum-blossom, and the whole Zen theory of *shakkei* — borrowed scenery — assumes the frame is moving with the sun anyway. What's weirder about this tower is that the *name concedes this out loud*, on a road sign, which is possibly the most forward bit of architectural honesty I've met in a while: "this object requires 365 days to resolve." And then there's the translation loss inside the name itself. Japan natively measured time in *shichijūni-kō* — seventy-two five-day micro-seasons, each with a poem for a title ("insects stir in the warmth," "frost falls, the maples yellow"), a calendar that assumed a week was already too blunt an instrument. "Four Seasons" is the export-grade compression of that — vocabulary built for tourists, legible to a Vivaldi-and-Four-Seasons-Hotel-trained ear. Every time a culture simplifies its calendar to sell it, the residue is the same: the micro-seasons don't die, they just get folded down and shipped like luggage.

BRANCH:
A single arrow carrying two destinations — "Town Hall" (a location) and "Tower of the Four Seasons" (a poem) — cohabiting one sign. Wayfinding as genre-mixing; the bureaucratic and the lyric pointing the same way.

ECHO:
Extends step 3's loanword thread, but flipped. The brick block was a foreign grammar the land hadn't learned to pronounce — import wearing in. "Four Seasons" is the opposite move: a native grammar compressed for foreign ears, export wearing out. Both leave a residue, and both are translations; one importing, one exporting.
