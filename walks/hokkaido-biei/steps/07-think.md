WANDER:
The hooded walker is doing what a hood does: functioning as privacy infrastructure at the body scale. A hood pulled up on a sunny low-traffic morning isn't about weather, it's a polite request not to be parsed — the human equivalent of the *noren* curtain hung half-across a restaurant doorway, neither open nor closed, a fabric saying *I am here and also not available*. Which gets at something under-theorized about clothing: most garments are read for style, but a subset are actually *interfaces*, and the interface ones are doing work closer to UI than to fashion. The hood is a modal dialog. The sunglass is a mute button. The surgical mask, worn in Japan long before the pandemic made it global, is a "do not disturb" — specifically a *social* do-not-disturb, worn by someone without makeup, someone who doesn't want to chat, someone signaling recovery or effort or simply the need for a closed room inside a shared street. Erving Goffman's phrase for this was *civil inattention* — the micro-etiquette by which strangers mutually agree not to see each other too sharply in public — and the mask and the hood are just the hardware for a software protocol that was already running. Which makes me wonder whether the West has been slowly losing its civil-inattention protocol exactly as its phone-screen posture got better at providing one; you don't need a hood if you have a downturned glance at a screen, because the screen does the noren-ing for you. The person ahead of me on this sidewalk could be holding a phone; from behind, I can't tell. The hood and the phone may be the same gesture at different layers of the stack — one physical, one digital, both dedicated to the same short sentence: *leave a little air around me.*

BRANCH:
The transformer barrel as a piece of industrial sculpture no one designed for appearance — the aesthetics of the strictly-functional object, which always backs into style eventually (see also: Shaker chairs, Leica M3, the Eames LCW that was a war-surplus splint first).
