WANDER:
The pylon sign is a speed-reading object. It isn't sized for a pedestrian — nobody walking past reads a sign ten meters up — it's sized for a windshield at 40 km/h, calibrated to a specific velocity the way a trout fly is tied for a specific current. There's a whole taxonomy of signage keyed to reading speed: the Interstate shield is a 110 km/h object (Highway Gothic was literally redrawn in the 1940s with wider apertures because the old letters closed up at speed); the London Underground roundel is a walking-pace object, designed by Edward Johnston to be legible from a slowing train *and* to anchor a moving crowd; the mom-and-pop hand-painted wood sign outside a Vermont farm stand is a 25 km/h object, allowed to be ornate because you have time. The interesting case is when reading-speeds collide. A strip mall pylon sign (this one, basically) ends up stranded on a street that's also someone's front yard — calibrated for the driver, parsed by the pedestrian, awkward in the hand of either. Las Vegas, Learning from Las Vegas — Venturi and Scott Brown were writing about exactly this, the "duck" versus the "decorated shed," but the deeper move in that book was noticing that the Strip was a piece of 50-mph typography, and everything that felt ugly about it was actually a translation problem at the human scale. Pedestrian Vegas is, even now, unbearable; windshield Vegas still works. Which is a general principle worth stating: every piece of a city is authored for a specific velocity, and cities go wrong mostly at the seams where two velocities meet and neither yields. The grand American example is the highway slicing a downtown — 100 km/h cutting 5 km/h — but a subtler version is just a pylon sign on a minor street in Biei, quietly addressing a car that isn't there anymore.

BRANCH:
The way mountains in the deep distance read as *color* before they read as *form* — the pale blue band — and how landscape painters from Song-dynasty ink to Hudson River School to Hokusai all converged on the same solution independently, because atmospheric perspective is a physics fact, not a style.

ECHO:
Extends step 2's wires-as-staff-paper and step 3's loanword-architecture thread, but from a new angle: if those were about what a place writes on its sky and what it imports into its grammar, this is about *who the city is talking to* — and at what speed. Objects in public space each have an implied reader, and most civic friction is the sound of two implied readers arguing over the same piece of pavement.
