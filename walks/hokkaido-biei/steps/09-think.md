SEED:
- A suspended traffic signal cantilevered out into the middle of the intersection on a long horizontal arm — the lamp housing black, the red/amber/green lenses staring down the road from an unusual height
- The orange-fronted retail block on the right, wide fascia in a flat saturated color, a Japanese convenience/hardware shop reading as a single long horizontal brushstroke against the sky
- A blue pedestrian-crossing sign on the near pole — white figure, blue field — the standardized ISO-ish pictogram that every country has agreed on with only small dialectical shifts

WANDER:
The cantilevered traffic light is a surprisingly recent object globally — the pedestal signal, four-sided, standing in the middle of the intersection, dominated American and Japanese streets well into the 1960s, and the decision to sling the lamp *over* the road on a long arm was a quiet concession that the car had won the right-of-way argument: the light no longer waited at the corner to be noticed, it now hung above the lane to be obeyed. You can track the entire 20th-century rebalancing of street power through that one bracket. Which makes me think of the overhead signage argument in airports — Paul Mijksenaar's work at Schiphol, where he insisted yellow-background signs should always be *above* the traveler's line of sight, because overhead signage reads as "authoritative route," while eye-level reads as "optional detail" — and suddenly Heathrow and JFK both quietly adopted the convention, and nobody noticed that a piece of graphic-design dogma from one Dutch consultancy had standardized the ceilings of global travel. Height is rhetoric. The pulpit, the judge's bench, the Jumbotron, the mihrab niche not raised but *deepened* to do the inverse move — every ritual space has figured out that vertical position does most of the work of "pay attention now." Which is maybe why hanging a gallery show at 57 inches on center, the museum standard, feels so neutral and also so evacuated: it's the height at which nothing is being claimed, the graphic-design equivalent of room-temperature. And there's a second thing going on with this signal specifically, which is that it's hanging out over an intersection in a town small enough that at 6am or 10pm there will be nobody at the light for minutes at a time, and it will still cycle — red, amber, green, red again — performing its office for an empty stage. There's something almost liturgical about infrastructure that keeps going when no congregation is present; the muezzin still calls at 4am over a sleeping city, the Vatican's Swiss Guard still changes at posts the Pope hasn't walked past in weeks, the BBC World Service's shipping forecast still reads out sea areas to trawlers that have mostly been replaced by GPS. The uselessness is the point — or rather, the *availability* is, and availability costs the same whether anyone shows up or not.

BRANCH:
The flat orange fascia of the shop as a single long word in the streetscape — the way retail colour in small Japanese towns tends toward one saturated hue per building rather than the multi-colour signage-pile of an American strip. Monochrome storefront as a kind of civic politeness.

ECHO:
Extends step 8's velocity-reader thread directly — that piece argued every object in the street has an implied reader and a speed; this one says every object also has an implied *height*, and height is doing rhetorical work underneath the graphics. Also keeps chasing step 4 and 5's question of what keeps running even when no one is watching: the flagger, the tulip, and now the signal cycling over an empty intersection — three variants of civic presence that refuse to be conditional on an audience.
