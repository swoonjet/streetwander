SEED:
- The zebra crossing in the near foreground, crisp white bars lying flat across the tarmac like rungs of a ladder nobody is climbing at the moment
- The foreground utility pole wrapped in a black-and-yellow hazard-chevron skirt, shin-to-bumper height, dressing the concrete in borrowed plumage
- The small red-and-yellow shop fascia at the far right corner, a flat saturated block holding the building's identity the way a single spot colour holds a logo

WANDER:
The chevron skirt on the pole is borrowing from a wasp. Not metaphorically — the yellow-black of *Vespula vulgaris* is the provenance, an aposematic pattern so effective at producing flinch response in vertebrates that human industry has spent eighty years pirating it for anything it wants a person to keep two feet away from: crane booms, the back of dump trucks, the lip of a loading dock in the Wolfsburg assembly hall, emergency-exit panic bars. What's interesting is the honesty gradient. The wasp is a true signal — squeeze one and it stings, the advertisement is underwritten by a real cost. OSHA tape around a concrete pole is closer to a hoverfly — *Episyrphus balteatus* wears the same stripes and cannot hurt anyone, a textbook Batesian mimic borrowing credibility from a species that pays the bill. Most industrial warning graphics are hoverflies. The pole is not going to bite; it's wearing the wasp pattern because your nervous system was tuned in the Pleistocene to read that specific spatial frequency and hasn't been re-tuned since. Then there's the richer case — Müllerian mimicry, where multiple actually-dangerous species converge on a shared signal and reinforce each other: monarch and viceroy wearing the same orange-black, each making the other's lesson stick in every bird that tries one. Traffic cones, hi-vis vests, amber strobes, the diagonal of a level-crossing arm, the hatched back of a garbage truck — these aren't independent design choices, they're a Müllerian complex. Each genuinely-unpleasant object lends weight to the shared dialect. Which is exactly why the system frays when somebody wraps hazard tape around a philodendron in a hotel lobby to keep guests from knocking it over: that's a hoverfly claiming wasp privilege, and with enough of them the wasp pattern itself begins to grey out. Signal inflation is a real cost, and the visual commons is genuinely shared — every frivolous use of a warning chromatic is a small tax levied against every serious one, which makes warning graphics a kind of currency with no central bank. The wonder is that the currency still spends at all; the worry is how much reserve is left.

BRANCH:
The zebra crossing itself — Slough, 1949, James Callaghan reportedly naming it — as the moment pedestrian priority had to be literally *drawn on the ground* because it couldn't be assumed anymore. The road as a palimpsest of rights you have to paint back in once you've lost them.

ECHO:
Extends step 0's grammars-of-warning thread along a new axis: step 0 asked *how* a culture chooses to warn (shout, apologize, withhold); this asks whether the warning is *honest or borrowed*, and what signal inflation does to a visual commons that nobody owns. Also faintly echoes step 1's Pareidolia Tax — both treat design as an economy where every expressive choice spends a shared budget.
