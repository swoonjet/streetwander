WANDER:
Pollarding — the tree's been cut back, year after year, to the same heel-knuckles, and the new growth is sprouting from each scar in the way only pollard regrowth does: vertical, furious, faintly embarrassed. What interests me is that the object is *a log of its own labor*; each knuckle is a winter somebody came by with a saw, and the tree is now a layered document of the municipal grounds budget — stratigraphy in wood. Compare a shaggy unmanaged roadside oak, which is one continuous event, one tree's life, to this one, which reads more like a filesystem of cuts, annually timestamped at the cambium. The analogy I keep reaching for is maintained software: a codebase refactored every quarter has exactly this candelabra profile, knuckles where the old interface was pruned back, fresh modules sprouting from each scar, the whole thing held in a shape that only exists because somebody keeps showing up — leave it alone for a decade and you get legacy, the wild oak, which the industry names disparagingly but which is, ecologically, just a tree that was allowed to finish its sentence. Everything that holds a shape against entropy is paying for that shape in recurring labor, and the shape is secretly a schedule: the gravel at Ryōan-ji raked each morning, the cricket pitch mown twice a week, the fingernails of a professional classical guitarist kept asymmetrical (right long, left short) against a very specific tone budget, the white paint on a crosswalk renewed before winter salt eats it. Pollarding is just that logic worn out-of-doors, on a longer clock, with a bigger pair of shears — a haircut is a six-week pollard, and the "shape" a person "has" is really the shape they're willing to keep returning to a chair to receive. I wonder whether the whole distinction between *objects* and *practices* is a trick of timescale: give any practice a slow-enough heartbeat and it congeals into a thing (this tree, this crosswalk, this lawn, this body), and give any thing a fast-enough observer and it dissolves back into the labor keeping it still.
