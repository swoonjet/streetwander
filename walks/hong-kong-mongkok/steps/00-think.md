SEED:
- The "STARBUCKS COFFEE" sign running the fascia of the building in plain cream sans-serif, directly above a neon red pharmacy sign in Chinese — 高峰藥房 — blazing in a different writing system, a different century, a different optical register
- The taxi's red rear stacked against the white van, both idling at a red light — three reds in the frame, none of them the same red
- The fisheye-barrel bow of the horizon, buildings leaning inward like they've been set on a spinning plate

WANDER:
The Starbucks sign is fascinating because it refuses to compete. It sits there in its unbranded Lucida-ish calm while the pharmacy below it is trying to set the street on fire with magenta and red tubing — and the Starbucks wins, precisely by not playing. This is the same trick a maître d' uses by wearing a black suit in a room full of tuxedos: the deliberate undertuning reads as status. I think about how Muji pulled the same move in Japan in the 80s, refusing the logo-as-event that Comme des Garçons and Issey were running, and how that restraint became its own kind of logo — the no-logo logo, which is a pun the Japanese word 無印 already contains. But the more interesting thing is that the restraint only works *against* maximalism; it needs the neon. APC on a street of APC stores would look like office supplies. The serif on a Celine bag performs restraint only because we can still feel the ghost of the old Phoebe Philo Céline-with-the-accent next to it — a typographic absence working as a dog whistle. Restraint is parasitic: it feeds on the noise it refuses. Which might be why minimalism always turns baroque the moment it succeeds, the way the original Swiss grid designers ended up producing the most ornate specimen books in history to prove they didn't need ornament.

BRANCH:
The three reds — taxi red, traffic-signal red, neon-tube red — as a study in how "red" is always a negotiation with its substrate: paint, gas, LED. Reds by medium.
