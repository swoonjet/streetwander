SEED:
- The "CO CO DUCK" storefront on the right — a backlit panel where the photographs of the duck and rice plates are larger and more saturated than the restaurant's own name, the food itself doing the work of the logo
- A small yellow regulatory sign at the left curb with the numerals "7" and "12" — parking restrictions stamped onto the asphalt, time written into place
- A thin hazy wedge of mountain visible at the vanishing point between two towers, the only piece of "outside" the street allows

WANDER:
The duck is the brand. CO CO DUCK has photographed its own product, blown it up larger than its name, and let the dish do the work that a wordmark would do in a Western chain — and this is the inverse of the Starbucks-siren-without-words I was thinking about a few frames back. The siren contract withholds; the duck contract overshares. One trusts you to fill in the meaning, the other trusts the meaning to stand on its own legs. Why has Western chain commerce so thoroughly rejected the photographic-menu strategy? Partly snobbery — pictorial menus code as "Denny's" or "tourist trap" — but I think it's also that Western brand semiotics is downstream of a culture that privileges the word over the image (in the beginning was the *logos*, not the photo of the loaf), and so the wordmark feels load-bearing in a way the photograph never does. Tokyo's plastic food samples — sampuru, an actual industry of resin sculptors making waxed-tempura that never spoils in the window — push the strategy further: not even a photograph, the *thing itself* (or its uncanny twin) doing the brand work. The whole apparatus assumes that the referent matters more than the story about the referent, which is a different ontology of commerce than Big Mac sameness. Though — the West *does* permit pictorial commerce in one place without sneering: real estate. Listings are pure photograph, and we accept it because the product is bespoke and unimaginable in advance. Maybe that's the rule. When the thing being sold is generic, the brand carries the value (golden arches, swoosh, siren). When the thing is specific and unrepeatable — a dish, a house, a used car — the photograph carries it and the brand recedes. The Hong Kong street is dense enough that you get both strategies within ten feet of each other, and neither one looks embarrassed.

BRANCH:
The wedge of mountain at the end of the street — Hong Kong negotiates with the impossibility of outward expansion the way Manhattan negotiates with its rivers. Geography as a constraint that becomes a grammar.

ECHO:
Extends step 3 — the skip-painter thread. The walk seems to keep asking: when is plain naming the move, and when is the gesture of refusing-to-name?
