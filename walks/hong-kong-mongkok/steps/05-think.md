WANDER:
The dashed line is the whole artifact. Its current grammar comes from GPS-trace aesthetics — the Strava squiggle, the Citymapper ribbon, the Airbnb-map pin-to-pin connector — but the convention is much older: the treasure map's dotted route, the Michelin guide's recommended drive, and, centuries back, the Peutinger Map of Roman roads, a strip where all of Gaul is flattened into a horizontal because what mattered was the cursus, not the countryside. Harry Beck's 1933 Underground diagram formalized the inheritance: once we accepted geography could be reduced to topology, the dashed line stopped being a *road* and became something like a *vector of interest* — an arrow of narrative attention drawn over any surface we wanted to make legible. What the hoarding is doing is subtler than it looks: it applies map-grammar to a photograph, which used to be a category error — you don't draw routes on pictures, you draw them on maps — and we've stopped noticing because Instagram Stories taught us swipe-paths, PowerPoint taught us callout arrows, every city-tourism campaign taught us a dashed stitch between capitals. The photograph-as-map is now so naturalized that a tourism image *without* an overlay feels weirdly mute, as if the landscape forgot to narrate itself. Small theory: every image on a hoarding is secretly a partially-annotated map now, even when it pretends to be scenery — which undermines "scenery" as a stable category, scenery just being a map waiting for its overlays. And then, one more turn — maybe the dashed line isn't about motion at all, maybe it's about *permission*: it says you are allowed to move through this. Which is exactly what the hoarding itself is doing at full scale, partitioning the street and granting passage to particular corridors, so the little blue dashes are the hoarding performing its own logic in miniature on its own surface, a building explaining itself to you in the handwriting of an app.
