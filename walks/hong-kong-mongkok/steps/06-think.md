WANDER:
The tailgate of the van is nearly vertical, and that verticality is the tell — a shape that has no proper English name because the market that would have named it never existed. Toyota Noah, Honda Stepwgn, Nissan Serena: the tall-van, a typology where design discipline runs backwards from the envelope inward, volume maximized before any gesture is made about "stance" or "posture." What looks like styling is a readout of an invisible legal surface — Japan's shako shomeisho (proof-of-parking) requirement, the kei-car tax class's tight footprint limits, the stacked-parking-tower height ceilings in Setagaya, the tsubo-by-tsubo pricing of a Tokyo lot that punishes every inch over the curb. You could tell the same story about a 1916-zoning Manhattan ziggurat, or the notched corners of a Shanghai shikumen, or the thin south-facing slabs Japanese apartments are strangled into by minimum-sunlight-hours rules — in every case the "form language" is just legislation compressed into steel and concrete, and the designer is choosing among the dialects a statute permits. Which is why it always feels slightly dishonest when someone at an atelier talks about a car's silhouette as if it emerged from a sketchbook; most of the sketchbook was already written by a planning commission nobody in the studio has read. The corollary is the weird one: deregulation produces duller forms, not freer ones. The mid-century American sedan got bigger and softer and less itself once CAFE rules slackened, while the kei-van keeps its crisp Euclidean weirdness because the law keeps pressing on it from all six sides — constraint as a species of ghost-designer, shaping without signing. You can read this backwards too: when a category of object looks suddenly coherent across a country, it's almost never a zeitgeist, it's a paragraph of code somewhere that nobody talks about. The Danish chair's proportions track postwar housing-subsidy ceiling heights; the British pub glass's shape is a 1699 excise law still doing its job; the Instagram square was the Kodak Brownie's ratio before it was anyone's aesthetic.

BRANCH:
The tall-van as a *room* rather than a vehicle — a piece of real estate you happen to drive. Japanese automotive ideology since the Odyssey: the car as living-room-on-wheels, which is a wholly different ontology from the American "driving experience."
