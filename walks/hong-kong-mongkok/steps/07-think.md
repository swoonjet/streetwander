WANDER:
"To be restarted" is the tell. It sits in a dialect I think of as International Brand English — not American, not British, engineered to read as none-of-the-above. The register has specific fingerprints: the passive voice used for warmth ("an experience to be had"), gerund-heavy noun phrases ("the rethinking of work"), definite articles dropped where a native speaker would keep them ("Home, reimagined"), and a faint TED-talk cadence crossed with concierge politeness. Uniqlo's global copy is the mature form — "LifeWear," "The Art and Science of LifeWear," sentences that feel translated even when they aren't, because they were composed in Tokyo rooms by writers who were aiming *through* English rather than from inside it. Jay Rubin's translations of Murakami sit in the same uncanny key; the narrator speaks Haruki-English, a dialect with maybe four million fluent readers and no country, neither Japanese in any literal sense nor American despite all the baseball caps and pasta. And this non-belonging is not a bug — it's the product. Localness reads as downmarket in luxury real estate: the hiker on a generic pine-forested slope is deliberately not Lion Rock because if the mountain were specific the aspiration would be trapped in a postcode. International Brand English does the same work at the sentence level that the stock photo does at the image level — it scrubs out the regional burrs that would tell you which actual human wrote this, so that the reader can be anyone, anywhere, with a credit card. It's beginning to look like the first widely-deployed language with no native speakers, a ceremonial tongue filling the slot Latin held in the medieval Church — except the ceremony is the soft opening of a fitness club in Mong Kok, and the priests are the brand consultancies in Shoreditch and Neukölln who authored the missal. A small theory: every global category develops its own "nowhere" dialect once its marketing crosses enough borders. Hotels have one (Aesop-adjacent, faux-essayistic). Airlines had a different one in the 90s, half pilot, half spa. Luxury residential has the flattest, most evacuated version — because residential is selling the promise of a self that doesn't yet exist, and grammar with too much origin would contradict the pitch.

BRANCH:
The hoarding as a window facing elsewhere rather than a wall facing here — how luxury development consistently renders its actual site as absent so the aspiration can be imported from a generic better-somewhere. The anti-vernacular as an architectural tactic.

ECHO:
Mostly a new thread, though it does fold back into step 1's bilingual-branding meditation. There the interest was in how two languages *diverge* (City Chain vs. 時間廊) and each tells a truer story by not agreeing. Here the interest is the opposite operation: a single language engineered to *diverge from all its origins at once*, to belong to no speech community, so that no reader feels too specifically addressed.
