### 1. MANDATE

Pursuant to Sec. 4(b) of the Administrative Review Act, the Office of Unsent Invoices was convened in the prior fiscal year with a narrow remit: to identify and, where feasible, *quantify* flows of value between the municipality, private undertakings, and members of the public which have measurable economic incidence but which are not presently reflected in any accounts payable, accounts receivable, licensing schedule, concession agreement, or tax return lodged with this department.

This memorandum covers the Office's pilot audit of a single corridor (hereafter "the Corridor"), running approximately 460 metres along a pedestrianised segment of the central historical district, undertaken between January and April of the current year.

### 2. METHODOLOGY

Seventeen (17) working days of field observation were supplemented by records requests to eight (8) departments, of which six (6) responded within the statutory period. The Office acknowledges that its methodology does not conform to generally accepted municipal accounting principles, as no such principles presently exist for the class of transaction under review.

### 4. AGGREGATE FINDINGS

The Office identifies, across the Corridor, an annualised notional flow of *unbilled transfers* estimated at between 1.8 and 4.6 million units, with wide confidence intervals owing to the absence of any prior instrument for measurement. The Corridor is found to be, on net, *solvent* in a sense not previously defined — that is, the transfers in each direction appear to approximately balance, though they do so across unlike parties, unlike time-horizons, and unlike categories, and any individual line item is unaccountable on its own.

The Office further observes that the transfers are not incidental to the Corridor's functioning; they are, by the estimates above, of the same order of magnitude as the Corridor's line-item expenditures, and in certain cases (UT-04.2, UT-04.5) may exceed them. The Corridor is therefore best understood as a *double-entry system* of which the Municipality presently records only one side.

### 5. REMEDIAL OPTIONS CONSIDERED

The Office considered four (4) remedial approaches:

**(a) Full invoicing.** Issue invoices, collect receivables, and bring the Corridor's accounts onto a transparent basis. *Rejected.* Field interviews with affected parties (couriers, newsstand operator, valet) indicated that the arrangements in question would not survive formalisation; the couriers would be displaced, the newsstand would discontinue the face-out practice in favour of a more defensible kiosk, the valet would relocate. The municipality would acquire a ledger entry in exchange for the destruction of the arrangement being entered.

**(b) Symbolic acknowledgement without collection.** Issue statements identifying each transfer but waive remittance. *Rejected.* The Legal Adviser observes that any enumeration, even non-binding, would invite challenge from parties currently unaware of their own participation in the system (see footnote 1). A statement of flows creates standing where none existed.

**(c) Cross-departmental reconciliation.** Circulate this memorandum internally so that departments (Parks, Heritage, Transport, Commerce) may adjust their own valuations to account for transfers identified here. *Partially adopted.* The Office will circulate to Heritage and Parks only. Transport and Commerce have declined the circulation.

**(d) No further action.** Close the file. *See Section 6.*

### 6. RECOMMENDATION

The Office recommends Option (d).

It is the Office's considered view that the soundness of the Corridor is a function of the fact that no party is presently in a position to sue, bill, tax, or account for any of the flows enumerated in Section 3; and that the chief utility of this memorandum is to exist, unread, on file, so that the Office may attest in any future inquiry that the audit was undertaken and its conclusions considered. The Office respectfully requests that the Director consider whether the circulation list at the head of this document might, on reflection, be narrowed further.

The Office notes that its own existence is an instance of UT-04.*n*: a line item whose justification is that no one has yet sent it an invoice, and whose effectiveness would be compromised if one were sent.

CLOSING:

I made an internal audit memorandum from an invented "Office of Unsent Invoices," tallying seven unbilled transfers along a generic civic corridor — plinth-as-break-room, tree-as-jali, rounded corner as unilateral gift, shutter-as-night-gallery, newsstand-as-common-attention, valet and shoeshine as commensal trades — and concluding, in proper bureaucratic form, that the office recommends its own findings be archived unread. The walk turned from semiotics of urban objects into an accounting question, and a deadpan memo was the only vessel that could hold the punchline seriously: the paradox that these arrangements are load-bearing *because* no one has billed them, so to audit them is to endanger them, so the auditor's final duty is to file the file and leave. The form does the arguing; the treasurer's voice is the theory.
