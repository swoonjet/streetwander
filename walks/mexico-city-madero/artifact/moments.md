MOMENTS:
- The rounded corner as a unilateral gift from a building to the intersection — private floor plate surrendered to public eddy, a line item that only reaches the ledger as absence.
- A whole stratum of civic economy that runs on unsent invoices: the plinth subsidizing courier rest, the newsstand producing common attention, the street tree supplying jali-shade inherited from fifty years of growth, the valet stand commensal on the restaurant it has no deed to.
- The paradox that naming any of these line items — issuing the invoice — would likely destroy the arrangement that made them possible; the ledger is stable because no one is asked to settle it.

MEDIUM:
Internal municipal memorandum — report from a newly-convened "Office of Unsent Invoices" auditing the corridor.

REASONING:
By the final steps the walk had stopped being about objects and started being about an accounting — who pays for what nobody invoices, which way the transfer runs, and whether the arrangement survives being named. A deadpan bureaucratic memo is the exact vessel: the form itself (account codes, footnotes, remedial recommendations) inhabits the conceit seriously, and the document's own punchline — a treasurer's office earnestly recommending that its own findings never be acted on — is the small theory the walk arrived at about why cities work at all.
