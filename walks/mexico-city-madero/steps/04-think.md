WANDER:
A six-armed candelabra lamppost presupposes gas. Gas mantles threw soft, weak light, so a municipal pole needed multiple heads to reach a tolerable lux at street level — four arms was the minimum, six was generous, eight was civic swagger. Electric lighting demolished that arithmetic in about a decade: one filament on a plain pole does the work of six mantles, and yet if you walk the heritage cores of cities modernized under Porfirio Díaz, or Haussmann, or late-imperial Vienna, you find street after street of ornamental iron carrying one LED per head, or worse, six decorative globes with the actual luminaire bolted on somewhere embarrassing behind them. The form has detached from function and turned into a kind of civic typography that says *you are on a street that ranks*. This is the same drift that kept "dial a number" alive through push-buttons and touchscreens, or that preserved the Tesla glove compartment named for a garment drivers stopped wearing around 1955 — skeuomorphism at municipal scale, the word longer-lived than the thing. But the interesting move isn't the lag, it's the inversion: once form is free of function it can be redeployed as *content*, and the candelabra lamp becomes a fluent little sentence meaning *historic, walkable, photograph this*. That's not dishonest; it's the city doing the work of self-description with the vocabulary it has, and the alternative isn't a rationalized sodium pole but the gas-station floodlight, which signifies just as loudly, only meaner. Six arms is the municipal equivalent of a serif — structurally redundant, doing the work of tone — and pedestrianizing Madero without installing candelabra poles would have been like setting a courthouse sign in Arial. The street needs the serif to finish the sentence about itself.

BRANCH:
The LED fixture bolted onto the side of the decorative globe — retrofit as a quiet admission that the historic form can no longer actually light anything, and that the ornamental cluster and the working lamp now cohabit the pole as two separate objects pretending to be one.

ECHO:
Extends the arc running through steps 0 and 3 — the Rappi cube as minimum-viable shop, the pedicab canopy as minimum-viable room — into a case where the gesture is doing cultural work that has nothing to do with its nominal function at all. A candelabra lamp doesn't light the street; it *tones* the street. Each step is circling a slightly different version of the same claim: that the semantic work of urban objects tends to drift free of the engineering that justified them, and that this drift is the thing worth watching, not a defect to correct.
