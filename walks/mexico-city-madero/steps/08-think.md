SEED:
- The newsstand kiosk on the right — a tall metal cube whose entire exterior is clad in magazine covers faced outward, a mosaic of small arguments from shin-height to eave.
- A dome and bell tower rising behind the lower storefronts in the middle distance, the kind of cupola that doesn't announce its building until you're almost under it.
- Red café umbrellas ranked along the left sidewalk, saturated enough to function as the block's color accent at a hundred meters and to do the block's wayfinding work for it.

WANDER:
The newsstand is retail in which the stock *is* the façade — a building type where the inventory and the envelope have fused into one surface, and every other shop looks timid by comparison. The *puesto de periódicos* puts every title face-out and lets the publishers' cover-design arms race do the merchandising for it. The magazine cover is, per square centimeter, the most pressurized layout work in graphic design: Condé Nast runs cover comps dozens deep, George Lois built Esquire's reputation on covers that picked fights with the reader instead of seducing him, and Alexey Brodovitch's Harper's Bazaar masthead was redrawn issue to issue to counter whatever else was riding the rack that month. What's strange, once you sit with it, is that the kiosk was already running the logic of the newsfeed decades before the feed existed — face-out the expected sellers, rank the also-rans upward until they vanish at the soffit, refresh on publication cycle — and the only structural difference is that the kiosk was *the same kiosk for every passerby*. The newsfeed is a kiosk in which every passerby sees a different façade. Which means the old kiosk was doing something the feed simply cannot: producing common knowledge of the day's public attention, the thing everyone on the block had just walked past whether they'd stopped or not. Its near-disappearance from most Anglophone cities is therefore less a retail story than a quiet erosion of a shared facing; the hold-out cities — Mexico, Rome, Paris, Buenos Aires — still pass strangers through the same morning compilation, and you can feel the difference in those sidewalks without being able to immediately name it. The National Geographic yellow border, negotiated over decades specifically to survive being jammed between two tabloids, was a contract with this urban form — a piece of graphic design that was actually a treaty with a display surface — and once that surface is gone the border is unemployed, even if it still wraps the same magazine.

BRANCH:
Logos as treaties with a specific display surface: the yellow border negotiated against the rack, the CD spine negotiated against the record-store alphabet bin, the app icon negotiated against a 6mm iOS corner radius. Design forms as rental agreements, disowned when the landlord dematerializes.

ECHO:
Extends step 0's thread about retail migrating onto surfaces — the Rappi cube as portable franchise — but twists it in a direction that drift didn't name. Step 0 asked how small a shop can get by climbing onto a body; this one notices that the *public* shop, the kiosk where every passerby read the same façade, produced a civic byproduct (common attention) that the personalized feed quietly deletes. Same preoccupation with commerce and surface; the stakes shift from labor to shared facing.
