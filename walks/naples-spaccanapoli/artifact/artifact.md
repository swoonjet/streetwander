ARTIFACT:

# A FIELD GUIDE TO UNSIGNED CIVIC PROTOCOLS

*Working edition. Pseudonymous. For private circulation among walkers.*

---

**Editor's note.** A city runs on two operating systems stacked on top of each other. The upper layer is the legible one: statutes, permits, lamp-post numbering, the map at the bus stop, the ordinance the council passed on a Tuesday in 1977. The lower layer is older and largely unwritten. It is maintained by habit, by proximity, by low-grade embarrassment, by fatigue, by the commercial need to see one's keys. Most of what makes a street actually work lives in the lower layer; when the lower layer fails, the upper layer cannot compensate, because the upper layer was never load-bearing to begin with — it was a decoration the lower layer had been quietly holding up. This guide is a working index of the lower layer. Entries are speculative and mutable. Where a definition meets its opposite it should be revised in pencil.

---

**Arm-height waterline** *(n.)*
The elevation at which graffiti stops — roughly 2.3 m, the reach of a standing adult with a can. Reliable enough to calibrate photographs against. Variants include the *kneeling exception* (a low band of tags implying a seated or child author) and the *reach stack* (where writers climb each other, betraying group authorship). The waterline is, among other things, a geological record of solo working conditions.

**Candle-quorum** *(n.)*
The minimum number of neighbors required to keep a street shrine lit. Below the threshold the bulb stays dead and the niche reverts to plaster. Short-term rentals reduce the quorum faster than almost any other demographic change, and faster than any demographic change anyone is currently measuring. Cf. *edicola decay curve*, *shame radius*.

**Cow-path lamp** *(n.)*
Any light not installed for wayfinding that nonetheless performs it. The bulb above a doorway at the bend of a dark passage, pulling feet in the correct direction because the eye is a gradient-follower. Difficult to simulate without the simulation showing through; see *patina uncanny*. The cow-path lamp smuggles incidental information (someone lives here, someone is home) inside its directional pull — which is why the simulated version, stripped of that payload, is never quite as persuasive as the original.

**Edicola decay curve** *(n.)*
The predictable sequence in which an unmaintained street shrine fails: first the flowers, then the candle, then the bulb, then the glass. The plaster outlasts them all. The exact inverse of the construction sequence, and a reasonable model for the decay of most small civic objects that run on distributed care.

**Fabric tell** *(n.)*
The most current public expression of a neighborhood's mood, found strung rather than built. Banners, flags, bunting, a ribbon on a streetlight, a black cloth on a door. A regime's first anxiety is almost always the fabric tell; it reads the pulse before the pulse has named itself. Regulators have historically gone after the wrong layer — statues, monuments — because they thought permanence equalled seriousness. Politics is serious on Tuesday. See *textile-time*.

**Fatigue settlement** *(n.)*
A governance protocol under which the enforceable position is held by whoever cares about it last. Observable in graffiti wars, shared-kitchen dish stacks, long Wikipedia edit disputes, condo-board email threads, and much else. Produces durable outcomes and essentially zero documentation. A large fraction of the governance humans actually live inside is this kind — protocols of weariness, not statutes.

**Haberdasher vanishing** *(n.)*
The phenomenon of a word and its referent collapsing together as a single linked ecological event. *Haberdasher, cordwainer, scrivener, edicola.* When the building type dies, the dictionary entry loses its habitat and degrades: first to quotation, then to footnote, then to ghost. Half of lexical extinction is really architectural extinction running a few decades ahead.

**Kerb privilege** *(n.)*
The surplus of use-value that unlabeled seating extracts from labeled seating. A proper bench makes a claim on the sitter (*linger here*); the kerb makes none, and therefore permits uses — waiting, pausing, mild loitering, phone triage — that a bench would foreclose. William H. Whyte documented the effect at scale in the 1970s and no city has fully designed for it since, because designing for it is the problem.

**Nightly blank page** *(n.)*
A surface that publishes itself through closure. The shopfront shutter becomes canvas exactly because the business is absent; the writing rights are, in effect, time-shared between merchant (by day) and writer (by night), by a contract nobody drafted and everyone honors. A rare case of a civic resource allocated by schedule rather than ownership.

**Palimpsest floor** *(n.)*
The condition in which no individual tag on a surface remains legible but the accumulated tagging reads, collectively, as a single texture. Graffiti has crossed into patina. The transition is undetectable in real time; it crossed over on a week nobody recorded. Analogous to the moment a reef stops reading as a graveyard of polyps and starts reading as an organism.

**Patina uncanny** *(n.)*
The slight dead quality of simulated accumulation — pre-distressed denim, the artfully untreated plaster of a chain boutique, a laugh track. Diagnostic symptom: the simulated article reads as the average of many real articles, which is precisely what no real article is. The uncanny is the fingerprint of the mean.

**Shame radius** *(n.)*
The distance within which the moral pressure of being-watched can still motivate unpaid maintenance. Shrinks rapidly with anonymity, turnover, and scale. Open-source projects possess one. Apartment buildings above a certain size have lost theirs. The candle goes out when the niche falls outside the radius; the kernel ships when the maintainer remains inside it.

**Sittable edge** *(n.)*
Any incidental step, low wall, planter lip, or kerb that furnishes seating without declaring that it does so. A generalization of kerb privilege. The sittable edge is the cleanest available form of the principle that affordance and invitation trade inversely: the more explicitly a thing is offered, the less fully it tends to be used. Corollary: half of good design is knowing when not to put a sign on something.

**Textile-time** *(n.)*
The underrated middle duration between stone-time (centuries) and paper-time (days). The six-month banner, the two-year awning, the bunting that outlasts its feast day by four seasons and comes down only during a funeral. Civic mood lives at this cadence. Regimes that have attempted to legislate mood have almost always ended up legislating fabric.

**Transaction-spillover lighting** *(n.)*
A street illuminated not by municipal fixtures but by the leakage from open shopfronts. An older protocol than the planned streetlamp. Runs on a feedback loop: the bright shop attracts feet, feet justify staying open, the open shop illuminates the next shop's customers. Fails instantly when commerce stops; the cobbles do not change, only the photons. Counter-case: the cloister, which has lit itself for a millennium on no transactional motive. Suggests that what actually lights a street is any sustained reason for someone to be there after dark.

**Witnessed neglect** *(n.)*
The motivational substrate underlying a surprising share of unpaid civic maintenance. Not ideology, not enthusiasm, not incentive design — merely the low-grade embarrassment of being the first neighbor, kernel maintainer, or group-chat member to let something fail. Operates only within the shame radius. Substantially more of the world runs on this than has been measured; the measurement would disturb the mechanism.

CLOSING:

I made a short reference book for a class of patterns that don't have entries anywhere — the arm-height bands, kerb seats, maintenance-by-embarrassment, transaction-spillover lighting, and the other ways a street actually functions under the thin crust of its legible rules. The glossary form is doing the work because these patterns deserve the dignity of being named, not anecdotalized, and because a field guide pretends to be a reference book while quietly advancing an argument — that the lower operating system of a city is real, inventoriable, and mostly invisible to its paperwork. The entries cross-reference each other because the patterns do.
