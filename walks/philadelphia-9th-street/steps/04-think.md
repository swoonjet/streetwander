WANDER:
The privacy blur on the painted face is the image doing something it doesn't know it's doing: Mapillary's face-detector has quietly classified a two-story portrait as a person and applied the same gauzian smear it applies to passersby, which means the model is working at the level of *features that compose as a face* and a competent painting passes that test. This is a weird accidental Turing test for the older medium — paint vouches well enough to fool the machine, which suggests the machine inherits an aesthetic canon from its training data and can't see past it. A cave painting wouldn't trip the filter; a Fayum portrait probably would; a Francis Bacon pope would depend on the model. So every automated image pipeline is, silently, drawing a line between representations-that-count and representations-that-don't, and the line is made of whatever faces showed up in the training set — which drifts, which gets retrained, which means the class of images legally visible on the internet is now downstream of engineering decisions nobody voted on. There's a second-order thing where, if the algorithm must blur painted faces to stay on the safe side of GDPR, then in a minor legal sense the mural's subject has become a person for privacy purposes even decades after death, and painted faces are now dim, unregistered citizens of the image commons, carrying a ghost-form of the right to be forgotten. I keep wondering about the inverse experiment: a classifier trained to fire only on *painted* faces, a detector for vouching, which you could run over every streetview tile to auto-unblur the murals and restore them to visibility. Benjamin's aura would come back as a model weight. And then — of course — the first adversarial attack against it would be some advertising agency painting their product photography in loose brushwork to slip past the filter and reclaim the streetview frame, at which point the whole edifice collapses back into commerce, which is where it always ends.

BRANCH:
Whether datasets are unacknowledged aesthetic canons — who decides what the machine learns "face" means, and can you read a decade's taste off an error distribution?

Extends step 3's "paint vouches" thread from the opposite direction: where step 3 argued that paint borrows seriousness from its obsolescence, this step notices that paint's seriousness leaks forward into algorithmic systems too — the machine can't tell, which is an accidental confirmation, but also a flattening. The protection the mural earns from Mapillary is not the consecration the neighborhood gave it; it's a much thinner, statistical cousin.
