MOMENTS:
- The aluminum bar sewn into Buzz Aldrin's moon flag — a designer refusing to accept that the medium (wind) has left the room, building a structural lie so the object will not look defeated on television.
- The canonical version of a thing is whichever surface most people happen to meet — the rear of the car the next driver stares at, the night-lit façade the architect never drew, the limp flag on a windless Tuesday — not whichever one the author intended as primary.
- Every medium eventually retires its errors into semantics: the hexagonal lens flare, the tape hiss, the magenta a 19th-century retina could not have named — defects laundered into the consensus grammar for "real."

MEDIUM:
A letter — from the engineer who designed the lunar flag's stiffening rod, written decades later to a younger designer.

REASONING:
The moon-flag image was the walk's deepest single noticing — design negotiating with an absent collaborator — and it kept rhyming with everything else: the night-lit cornice performing daylight it doesn't have, the rear taillight signature performing a hood ornament for nobody, the magenta façade performing a wavelength that doesn't physically exist. A letter is the right vessel because the conjured thing is a confession in retrospect: an old engineer watching his one small admission metastasize into a profession's whole posture. Letter form lets the speaker contradict himself, lets him notice in real time that the bar he built in 1969 became the substrate of every dashboard, every demo loop, every showroom preview — without anyone deciding it should.
