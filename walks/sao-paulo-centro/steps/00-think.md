WANDER:
The cart shouldn't be there. Supermarket carts are engineered to stay on premises — some now carry a magnetic sensor in one wheel that slams to a lock at the lot's perimeter, a geofence rendered as physical refusal. And yet they migrate, constantly, and end up being one of the most repurposed industrial objects of the last century: bike trailer, portable smoker, laundry conveyor, mobile storage for the unhoused. In 1988 Krzysztof Wodiczko built the Homeless Vehicle Project — a shopping cart redesigned into a pod with a sleeping canister and water tank — and the art world called it sculpture; the actual carts on actual streets were already doing that same design work, unsanctioned, just without a curator. Any object that is cheap, wheeled, and not fully owned will be captured by a secondary economy faster than its original market can react to the loss — bike-share bikes belong to the same species, which is why they end up in canals, in trees, wedged into fences as racks for non-share bikes. The sanctioned lifecycle assumes an owner; the unsanctioned lifecycle only requires a user. You can see the same pattern in software when a library gets forked more than it gets starred, or in language when a slang word escapes the subculture that coined it and nobody can get it back. An object's "intended" use is just the first draft; the second is written by whoever has a problem and happens to be nearby.

BRANCH:
The sensor-locked wheel as a category — where else do we encode ownership as a refusal to move?
