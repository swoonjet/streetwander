WANDER:
Two red circles on one sightline: the "50" and the barred-E No-Stopping. They are doing wildly different grammatical work with the same visual apparatus. The 50 is a positive command with a numeric variable — a function signature, essentially, `limit(50)` — infinitely reusable because the payload swaps. The No-Stopping is a negation plus a pictograph of the thing negated, closer to a sentence than a function, and it scales badly: every new prohibition demands a new glyph inside the bar, and the glyph has to survive being read at 60 km/h through rain. Traffic signage is one of the rare design systems where the world actually adopted a strict typology — Vienna Convention 1968, red for prohibition, blue for mandatory, yellow diamond for warning — and it mostly holds across borders the way few other visual languages do. Compare it to plug sockets, which splintered into at least fifteen incompatible national standards because nobody convened a Vienna for them in time; or to date formats, where YYYY-MM-DD lost to habit despite being obviously correct. The window for global standardization seems to close very fast, maybe one or two decades after a technology becomes common, after which every installed base becomes a constituency with veto power. Which suggests the question isn't whether a standard is good but whether it arrived before the lock-in — HTML got there, CSS units didn't, emoji barely did and only because Unicode had already paid the diplomatic cost. The 50 on that pole is enjoying the fruits of a 1968 treaty nobody has to think about anymore, which is what success looks like in standards work: invisibility.
