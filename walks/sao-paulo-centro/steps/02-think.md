WANDER:
The tunnel mouth has eaten the cars, which is a sensor problem — 12 stops of dynamic range against a scene that wants 18 — but it's also a biology problem the sensor is just miming. The human retina takes roughly a minute to light-adapt and five to fully dark-adapt, and drivers approaching a tunnel in daylight get neither luxury: they get what highway engineers call the "black hole effect," where the mouth looks like solid pitch because your iris is still calibrated for sky. The response is a category of invisible civil engineering called threshold and transition zones — the first stretch inside a tunnel is deliberately over-lit to match outdoor luminance, then stepped down over a few hundred meters following ratios specified in CIE guidance (the approach-zone "1:20 rule," the transition-zone decay curve). You have been driven through thousands of meters of engineering whose sole purpose was to buy your retina time, and you noticed none of it, which is the whole point. Film knows this too: Gordon Willis shot the opening of *The Godfather* knowing your eyes would need a couple of seconds to find Brando in the dark office, and he used that delay as scene-making — the audience's pupil work becomes part of the grammar. Kubrick went the other way in *Barry Lyndon*, chasing a Zeiss f/0.7 lens because film stock didn't dark-adapt like eyes do and he wanted the candlelit rooms to actually read as candlelit. Both are cases of a fast artifact (cut, exposure) waiting on a slow system (vision) and either accommodating it or weaponizing it. The more I turn this over the more it looks like threshold zones are everywhere a fast thing meets a slow one: skeleton screens on web apps give the user's mental model a transition zone before the real interface lands; a good lecture opens with five minutes of orientation that the expert finds obvious and the novice needs to dark-adapt into; even a well-designed onboarding email drip is a luminance ramp. Bad design is the design that forgets the recalibration takes wall-clock time — it assumes the user's pupils are already dilated and cuts straight to the dim room.

BRANCH:
Weaponized thresholds vs. accommodating thresholds — Willis and Kubrick both used the adaptation delay, but one hid it and one flaunted it. There's a whole taxonomy there.
