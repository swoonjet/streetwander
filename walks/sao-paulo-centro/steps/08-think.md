WANDER:
The brake light is the point. For nearly a century the back of a car was an afterthought — a number plate, a badge, two cheap bulbs doing the legal minimum — while every dollar of styling effort went into the grille, which was the half that sold the car in a showroom photograph. Then two things happened at once: LED arrays dropped in price to where you could *draw* a light rather than install one, and everyone else on the road began spending the majority of their driving time staring at somebody's trunk. The rear got promoted. Porsche's four-dot band, the Audi full-width blade, the Volvo vertical column, the Charger's racetrack, the Hyundai Ioniq's 8-bit pixel cluster — these are the primary signatures a car actually broadcasts in the wild, because nose-on views only happen in adverts and in parking lots. Brand identity migrated to the surface that gets looked at most, which sounds trivially correct and yet almost every industry gets it wrong for decades before self-correcting: Apple didn't rotate its laptop logo right-side-up (for the *room*, not the user) until the mid-90s, GPU makers didn't design card backplates until open-case PCs made them visible, and shampoo bottles on hotel shelves still waste their pretty front on the wall. The lingering surface, not the hero surface — wherever the gaze actually accumulates over time — is where identity has to live, and the gap between the two is where marketing still hasn't caught up to ergonomics. Bands only started caring about merch design after streaming killed the album cover, because the cover had been the lingering surface and now the t-shirt was. The awkward inverse is that industries still controlling both surfaces — who decides what you see first versus what you see most — quietly weaponize the gap: nutrition labels live on the back because the regulator couldn't force them to the front, and the front goes to the claim.

BRANCH:
Front-of-package vs. back-of-package as a regulatory problem — which surface the law conquers first tells you which regime is winning that decade.

ECHO:
Rhymes with step 4's re-performance thread from a different angle — there the canonical version of a building shifted from day to night because night is when most people see it; here the canonical brand surface shifted from front to rear because rear is what other drivers mostly see. Both steps keep arriving at the same rule: the authoritative version of a thing is whichever surface has accumulated the most looking time, not whichever one the author drew first.
