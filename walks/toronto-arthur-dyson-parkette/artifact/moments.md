MOMENTS:
- Marcescence as an organizational pathology: the abscission layer never forms, the deprecated thing rides along sealed in place, and only insistent new growth from the other side will pop it off.
- Three failure modes for persistence — vestigial (function ended, form remains), marcescent (exit was scheduled and didn't fire), spolia (dead matter recruited *because* the work was already done) — and the suspicion that organizations can name only the first.
- Forensic minimalism: from the curb, you can't tell intent from residue, and the warmth you feel for "leftover space" may just be a misattribution.

MEDIUM:
A leaked internal memo announcing a sunset that quietly admits, by its final paragraph, that the sunset is not going to happen.

REASONING:
The marcescent-leaf observation was the sharpest thing the walk produced — the recognition that organizations can't reliably issue release signals, and that the only force which actually clears dead matter is new growth insistent enough to break the seal from the other side. A taxonomy or field-guide would domesticate that insight; a memo *enacts* it. The form lets the document perform its own subject — a sunset announcement that is itself marcescent, written by someone who realizes mid-paragraph that announcing an exit is not the same as having one. Bureaucratic prose is the right register: it's where abscission failures actually live.
