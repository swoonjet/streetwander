The Lineage of the Red Corner Burst

Six stations of one persistent diagonal.
6stations

From a tabard at Agincourt to a parking sign at Yonge & Carlton: the same red diagonal, six contexts, six audiences. The form remains; the war it was bred for ends; the form is reassigned. This is the family tree the speaker recites in the monologue when the Cavalier in stall 14 won't move.

c. 1415
Agincourt

The heraldic corner I.

Heraldry · battlefield · personal arms

A diagonal stripe in red, sewn onto the upper corner of a knight's tabard. Reads at a hundred paces. Distinguishes one armoured man from another in a press of armoured men.

I am So-and-So's; do not strike me.
c. 1955
Cincinnati

The detergent burst II.

Mid-century packaging · NEW! NOW WITH MORE!

The first generation bred for shelf combat. The corner of the box is the eye's last position before it leaves the box. Putting the burst there is the cheapest possible way to ambush attention. Every other word is an exclamation.

I am the new one; choose me, not the box next door.
c. 1992
Anywhere

The shelf at war III.

Late commercial · saturated category · diminishing returns

Twenty boxes wide, each with a corner burst. The ambush has been generalised; the eye has nowhere to be ambushed from, because everywhere is the corner of something. The form is dying of its own success.

I am the new one. So is everyone.
c. 2014
Yonge & Carlton

The parking ribbon IV.

Civic notice · monopoly lot · a soldier whose war ended

A diagonal red ribbon bolted to a parking sign at a property line that the bylaw has already drawn. There is no Green Q across the street to undercut. There is no choice to lose. The ambush continues, by reflex, at the back of a man unlocking a Subaru.

24 HOUR. (At nobody, in particular.)
c. 2018
Civic infrastructure

The civic index V.

Pure function · code-reserved colour · grab-here red

The same diagonal, in a wavelength reserved by code, on a fire connection. Here the gesture has meant precisely one thing for a hundred years and never had to compete for attention. Grab here. Grab here, and only here. A red civilians are not allowed to spend.

Hose connection; this is not a metaphor.
c. 2026
A storefront window

The civic mannerism VI.

Vernacular default · a shape that means "sign"

A retail SALE sticker peeling from a glass shopfront. The diagonal has stopped being a weapon and started being a costume — the way we draw a mark when we want it to read as a notice. The form has eaten the function and become the category icon for itself.

I am the way this kind of message looks.
