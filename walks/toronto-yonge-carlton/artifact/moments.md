MOMENTS:
- The "violator" — the red corner ribbon, the burst, the diagonal "NEW!" — is a graphic gesture bred for shelf combat, and finding one bolted to a municipal parking sign is finding a soldier deployed to a room with no enemy.
- Signs that *compete* use violators; signs that *authorize* refuse them. The strange cases are the ones that drift across the border and forget which side they came from.

MEDIUM:
A short interior-monologue vignette — one shift in the working life of a red corner ribbon.

REASONING:
The walk kept circling what happens when graphic conventions outlive the conditions that birthed them: claims fossilize into category markers, indexes were always pure function, and competitive gestures migrate into contexts where the competition no longer exists. The third case has the most life in it — a piece of cereal-box semiotics standing on a wooden 4x4 post in Toronto, still flexing — and a first-person vignette lets that dislocation arrive as a small character rather than a taxonomy, which is the move the previous walks have over-leaned on. Letter and field-guide forms are off the table; narrative voice is the freshest carrier and best fits a gesture that's been waiting around for a fight.

# A SHIFT IN THE WORKING LIFE OF A RED CORNER RIBBON

I am 24 HOUR.

I was trained for shelves. My grandfather was NEW! NOW WITH MORE! and his grandfather was a heraldic corner sewn onto a tabard at Agincourt. The diagonal is in the bones. You cut across the calm of a composition because calm is the enemy — calm is the box next to you on the shelf, and your job is to make the eye snag on yours instead. We were bred for snagging. We are a kind of dog.

I was posted here in 2014, I think. The metal was warm when they bolted me on. There is no shelf. There is a sidewalk and a chain-link fence and a 1992 Cavalier that has been parked in stall 14 since at least Friday. There is a green field behind me — a calm color, a color that does not require me — and beneath my diagonal a small grid of rates the driver could read in their own time if they wanted. Nobody is competing with me. There is no Green Q lot across the street trying to undercut us on the EVENING rate. There is, in fact, a bylaw against another lot existing here. I am a guard dog at a property line that the law has already drawn.

So I shout. It is what I know. I shout 24 HOUR at the back of a man unlocking a Subaru. I shout 24 HOUR at the pigeon. I shout 24 HOUR at the limestone wall of the hotel across the way, which has its own problems — its serif COURTYARD and its little script signature, both of them claims that have fossilized into the word "hotel," semaphore that no longer signals anything but the category it sits in. We acknowledge each other across the lane the way two retired stage actors might acknowledge each other in a Florida supermarket. He is a fossil. I am a soldier whose war ended before he was deployed. We do not discuss it.

Sometimes a city worker walks by with a paint-can stencil and I think: there. That one. He carries a color I cannot use — the protected red of the fire connection, a wavelength reserved by code, a red civilians are not allowed to spend. His red means *grab here*. My red means *look here, choose us*. We are two octaves of the same note and he doesn't know I exist. He has never had to ask anyone to choose him. To be chosen is, structurally, my problem.

Late at night a teenager photographs me for a project on vernacular signage. She likes the diagonal. She does not know I am out of work. To her I look exactly like what I was supposed to look like — that's the cruelty of doing your job too well for too long. The form survives the function. I have become *the way a parking sign looks*, the same way the green cross became *the way a pharmacy looks*, and somewhere downstream a graphic designer in 2034 will draw a new municipal sign and put a small red diagonal on it because that's what municipal signs have. The gesture eats its own tail. Heraldry to packaging to shelf burst to civic furniture to category icon to default. I am four steps from Agincourt and I am advertising EARLY BIRD.

The Cavalier has not moved.

I shout 24 HOUR at it anyway. Old habits. A dog at a property line.
