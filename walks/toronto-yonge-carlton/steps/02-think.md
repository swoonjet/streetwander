WANDER:
The red corner ribbon is the thing that pulls — and the term for it, in packaging design, is "violator" (or in supermarket trade, "burst"): a graphic that violates the calm of a composition to flag one fact loudly. A cereal box is essentially nothing but violators stacked on violators — NEW! NOW WITH! +20% FREE! — and the form descends from the dog-eared bookmark and, further back, from the heraldic corner badge sewn onto a tabard. What's strange is finding a violator on a parking sign, because the violator was *bred* for shelf competition: Tide pods need to beat All, so the box shouts; a Green P lot at 28 Wood Street has no shelf-mate and no convert to win. Maybe the red ribbon is doing signage work in retail clothing — flagging the one fact a driver decides on first, *is this lot even available right now*, before they care about the rate — and "24 HOUR" earns the diagonal because availability outranks price as a question. Or maybe it's just cargo cult, a gesture that originated in competition migrating happily into contexts without any, the way every municipal website now has a hero image and a CTA button even though nobody is choosing between the Toronto tax portal and a rival vendor. There may be a clean typology hiding underneath: signage that *competes* uses violators (menus, storefronts, end-caps), signage that *authorizes* refuses them (FHWA Highway Gothic on green-on-white, the London Underground roundel, regulatory plaques) — and this Green P sign sits awkwardly across that boundary, a public-private hybrid that needs to flag itself to passing traffic without having a competitor to flag against. The clean test case is the federal highway sign, which has no italics, no bursts, no diagonal anything, because once you're on the road you've already chosen it; the violator only shows up where choice is still live, where there's still something to lose to a neighbor.

BRANCH:
What's the violator's equivalent in software UI — the red badge on the app icon, the "NEW" pip on a menu item — and is it migrating the same way, from competitive surfaces to authorizing ones?

ECHO:
Extends the running thread about graphic gestures outliving their original context. Step 0 had a boutique typeface ported onto a chain hotel; step 1 had a brass fire connection that was *never* a claim, only ever an index; this one is a retail-shelf convention (the corner burst) ported onto civic-adjacent signage where the competitive pressure that birthed it doesn't exist. Three rooms in the same house: claims that fossilize, signs that were always pure function, and conventions that wander across category lines and stick.
