ARTIFACT:

A letter from Margaret Eames, retiring clerk, to her successor.
Left in the top right drawer of the desk, with the keys.

—

Dear Wren,

The keys are in the envelope. The big one is the vault. The small brass is the file room. The one with the green tape opens nothing anymore but I never had the heart to throw it out — when you find what it goes to, please write me.

I want to tell you the part of the job nobody trains you for. The forms you'll learn in a week. The council in a month. The rest takes longer, and most of it isn't written down, so I'm writing some of it down now.

The first thing is: most of what this office does is record what stopped. Permits expire. Variances lapse. Tenants move out. A roof gets replaced and the old shingles never get a paragraph; a building burns and we have a folder six inches thick. The ledger isn't a list of what the town is. It's a list of what the town has had to *notice*, and the town only notices interruption. You will catch yourself, in your first year, thinking the files are a portrait. They aren't. They are a casualty list that occasionally records a birth.

This sounds bleak. It isn't. Once you accept it, the work gets easier, because you stop looking for the building and start looking for the gap. A lot that nobody has filed on in eleven years is doing something — somebody is paying tax on it and waiting. A street that hasn't had a sidewalk variance in two decades is doing something — the merchants there have agreed, without anyone calling a meeting, that they like it the way it is. The absence of paper is paper. Read the shelves for what isn't there.

You'll find a binder marked Closed Apertures in the south cabinet. It's mine. I started it in '94 after Don Hewitt at the assessor's office told me the town had been losing taxable openings — windows filled in, doors blocked, dormers shingled over — at a rate the appraisal forms didn't account for. I kept track. There are two hundred and eighteen entries, with photographs from the second decade onward. I do not know what the binder is *for*, exactly. But I have used it. When a developer comes in and tells you a building has always looked the way it looks, you can sometimes reach behind you and disagree with him. You won't always be right. But the disagreement is its own service.

The second thing is harder. You have to learn to look at this town with the color taken out.

I mean this practically. When I am trying to decide whether something is true — whether a complaint has merit, whether a proposal is what it says it is, whether the new sign on Victoria is going to age into the streetscape or shout at it for ten years — I take the color out in my head. I imagine the corner in pencil. The signs go grey, the awnings go grey, the cars go grey, and what's left is the hierarchy: which lines are doing the work. If the proposal still makes sense without its paint, it will probably be all right. If the only argument for it is the color, send it back. The color is what people show you when they want you to stop seeing the shape.

A surveyor I knew in Belleville called this the *squint test*. He said any plan that survives a hard squint is a plan, and any plan that doesn't is a brochure. He retired the year you were born, but the test is good and you can have it from me.

The third thing has to do with time, and I am not sure I have it phrased right yet, so I will tell it to you sideways.

Every surface in this town is operating on at least three clocks. The wall is on the slow clock; it was built in 1889 and it expects to be here in 2089. The painted name on the wall is on the medium clock; tenants come and go in years. The vinyl in the cabinet above the painted name is on the fast clock; it can change in a morning if the printer at Foxboro has the stock. When somebody complains to you about the look of something, the first question — before anything else — is *which clock is the complaint about*. Most disputes in this office are people on different clocks insisting the others are wrong. The vinyl people think the painted-name people are stuck in the past. The painted-name people think the vinyl people are tourists. The wall people, who are mostly dead, do not vote.

Your job is partly to make sure the slow clock keeps a quorum. It cannot defend itself. The fast clock will always have an agenda item; the slow clock is just *there*, and you have to speak for it sometimes when nobody else will. Not by saying no to everything new. By making sure that whatever new thing is added is added in a way that the next slow surface doesn't have to apologize for.

A practical rule: if a proposal is reversible, you can usually approve it. If it is not, take a week. Walk past it three times. Take the color out.

The last thing is small. There is a thermos in the bottom drawer that the previous clerk, Ed Tully, left for me in 1986. I never used it; I have my own. He left a note inside saying he hoped I would have a good forty years. He was off by two. Leave the thermos for whoever follows you and write a better note than mine.

The job is mostly listening to the building tell you what it has stopped doing, and not letting anyone fill that quiet too quickly.

I'll miss the desk.

Yours,
M. Eames
April 30


CLOSING:
A retirement letter from a small-town clerk, handed across the desk with the keys, that translates the walk's three conjured ideas — reading a place through its bounded subtractions, the picturesque-in-reverse / squint-as-seeing, and stacked civic timescales — into operating wisdom for a successor. The form is doing work the essay couldn't: the methods land as inheritance, not theory, because they're being passed by someone who used them on rooms that aren't ours. No street, no Tweed, no panorama; just the way a long-tenured eye learned to look, written down because she finally had to.
