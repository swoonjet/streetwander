MOMENTS:
- A subtraction kept legibly bounded — bricked window, blind party wall, fenced empty lot, deadhead trailer — speaks louder than a surface that was never opened. The town's truth lives in what it's stopped doing.
- The picturesque-in-reverse: a Claude glass, a desaturated mockup, a black car hood reflecting a streetscape stripped to silhouette — reduction tools that precede the act of seeing, devices that decide what to remove first.
- Civic surfaces operate at stacked timescales (brick / painted name / leasable vinyl) and the honest layer is rarely the loudest.

MEDIUM:
A letter from an outgoing small-town clerk to her successor, on the methods of the office.

REASONING:
The walk kept circling buildings that wore their edits — the bricked aperture, the blind flank, the empty lot, the empty cabinet — and the question wasn't really architectural but epistemic: how do you learn to read a town through its negative space? A letter from a forty-year clerk to the person who'll inherit her desk lets that competence land as practical knowledge instead of theory — she's trying to hand down a way of looking, the same gesture as Claude's glass or a designer's greyscale toggle, and the form (intimate, professional, slightly elegiac) carries the conjured thing without ever needing to show the street.
