WANDER:
The bricked-up windows are the thing — not bricked exactly, smoothed over with a paler concrete that sits a half-inch shy of the surrounding stone, so the openings still cast a faint shadow at the lintel. Nobody filled them flush. That gap is the tell: it's cheaper to set the patch back, but it also confesses the act. A window that has been stopped is louder than a wall that was never opened, the same way a redacted line in a document is more conspicuous than the line that was simply never written. Insurance, probably — or the upper floor became storage and the tax assessor counts apertures. Either way the building wears its own edit. There's a category for this in typography: the slashed zero, the dotted i — marks added to *resolve* an ambiguity that an unmarked form would leave open. The blanked window is the opposite move, an unresolution; it removes the function but keeps the punctuation. I think about how Notre-Dame de Ronchamp has those deep splayed window-wells where the glass is the smaller event and the masonry is the composition — Le Corbusier understood that a window is mostly its hole, and the hole keeps speaking even when the glass is gone. Renovation as palimpsest. Maybe every long-lived facade is a stack of decisions someone got tired of explaining.

BRANCH:
the economics of apertures — Georgian window taxes, the way a tax on light produced a vernacular of bricked panes across England, and what we would build if floor area were free but openings were billed.
