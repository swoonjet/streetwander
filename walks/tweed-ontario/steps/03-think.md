WANDER:
The CHINESE sign is the thing — a blade sign with just the one word, no restaurant name, no "Wong's" or "Lucky Garden," not even "restaurant" appended. Just the category. Which tells you something about the size of the town: when there is exactly one of a thing, you do not name it, you *label* it. The Chinese place. The hardware store. The doctor. Linguists call this the definite generic, and it's the same logic that lets a programmer call a loop variable `i` in a five-line script and forces them to call it `customerOrderTransactionIndex` in a fifty-thousand-line codebase — ambiguity grows with the population, and names have to do more work to disambiguate. There's a quiet corollary in commercial naming: small-town businesses are allowed to be common nouns ("Bakery," "Dollar Store"), urban competitors must invent ("Boudin," "Dollarama"), and global competitors must invent something pronounceable across fourteen languages and trademark-free across all of them — Häagen-Dazs, a name made of nothing, designed to mean nothing in particular so it could mean one specific thing. The sign-painter who made the Tweed blade sign was operating under a much shorter constraint set: one Chinese restaurant in town, a piece of red enamel, a vertical surface, brief writes itself. I keep wondering whether there's a population threshold at which the categorical sign breaks — the Dunbar number for commerce, the moment when "the Chinese place" stops resolving and someone has to specify "the one on Victoria Street," at which point names sprout and the whole indexical economy collapses into a brand economy. Toronto's response to that collapse is to over-correct with hyper-regional precision — Northern Dumpling Kitchen, Xi'an Cuisine, Mother's Dumplings — granular sub-naming as a market signal once "Chinese" has gotten too broad to mean anything at all. The same word does not survive the scaling, even though the cuisine does.
