WANDER:
The signals are the thing — not the lights themselves, but the *cable* they ride on, a span wire borrowed between two utility poles instead of a proper mast arm. A mast arm is a commitment: you pour a foundation, you erect a galvanized cantilever, you've declared this corner a permanent intersection. A span wire is renting air — a length of steel cable strung between objects that already had jobs, with the lights hung off it like wet socks on a clothesline. The whole installation is *tensile*, not compressive, and tensile structures cost less because they don't need to resist being pushed over, they only need anchors that can pull. You can find the same logic in the rope bridges of the Inca *q'eswachaka*, rebuilt every June from braided grass, where the engineering case isn't permanence but cyclical re-tensioning; or in a hammock, which is the cheapest possible bed because it makes the trees do the structural work. What I keep circling is that the *air above the street* used to be a public room — you could string a banner across Main Street for the harvest festival, you could hang Christmas garlands pole-to-pole, you could run a streetcar wire — and now in most cities that volume has been quietly enclosed by liability law and by the fact that nobody owns it clearly enough to grant permission. The traffic signal is one of the last legitimate inhabitants of that vanished commons. Compare a cable-car city like Wellington or Valparaíso, where the air is still actively in use, to a place like Toronto where the trolley wire was removed and the sky over Yonge Street is now empty in a way that nobody mourns because nobody remembers it was ever full. There's a category here I'd call *negative infrastructure* — the things you used to be allowed to do above a street, only legible by what you're not allowed to do anymore.

BRANCH:
*tensile cheapness* — when a structure works by borrowing forces from objects that already exist (span wire, hammock, awning, lean-to), and what civic services would be possible if we accounted for borrowed structure rather than insisting every new function bring its own steel.
